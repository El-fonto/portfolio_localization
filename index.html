Honda CHA125 — Technical Localization for a Cross-Border Audience

The Honda CHA125 series was commissioned for a specific audience: Hispanic mechanics working in California and in Baja California Norte — Mexicali and Tijuana. The documents needed to be fully self-sufficient in Spanish. The chief mechanic who coordinated the technical review had working familiarity with English but not full command of it, which meant every term had to stand on its own without any expectation that a technician would cross-reference the source.

Spanish expands English by roughly 20–25% in technical prose. Across 42 manuals, this produced systematic layout overflow — specification tables, warning callouts, and section headings all exceeded their original frames. After completing the translation in SDL Trados, the layout corrections were applied in Adobe Illustrator, including a full re-pagination of each document. Page numbers shifted throughout to accommodate the longer text while preserving the manual's navigational structure.

The challenge was not finding the right word — it was fitting it into a space designed for a shorter one, without losing the precision a mechanic's safety depends on.

Terminology Management — Espanso + Python Workflow

Maintaining term consistency across 42 manuals — thousands of segment pairs over several months — required more than a static glossary. Espanso was configured as a live autocorrection layer running alongside Trados, catching typing errors and terminology inconsistencies before they reached the translation memory. Two language databases run in parallel: one in English, one in Spanish.

Both databases are managed through a Python script that pushes new entries directly to the Espanso YAML configuration files. When a term is confirmed — from the SME during review or from a cross-reference in Trados — it goes into the script, which updates both databases in a single run without requiring any manual file editing. The same system serves three independent domain glossaries: automotive (Honda), medical, and literary.

You Are Magic — Literary Translation with Internal Rhyme

The challenge in translating Tú eres la magia was not vocabulary — it was prosody. Every page in the original Spanish carries internal rhyme that works quietly but purposefully, giving the text a rhythm appropriate to its subject: space, balance, play. A word-for-word English version would have preserved the meaning and lost everything else.

For several passages, the author was consulted directly to establish what each phrase was meant to do — its tone, its emotional register, the image it was meant to leave. Only after that conversation was it possible to attempt an English version that honored those intentions rather than simply transcribing them. The sample below shows one such passage: a sequence about the eternal balance game, where the Spanish rhyme scheme had to be rebuilt from scratch in English, not translated.
